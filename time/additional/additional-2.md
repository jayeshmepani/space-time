The Nine types of Measures of Time
There are many different cycles which can be used to keep track of flow of time. There are 9 of them which are useful for different purposes.
The 9 types of reckoning time are (1) Brahma (Braahma), (2) Deva (Daiva), (3) Manu (Maanava), (4) Pitrya, (5) Saura, (6) Savana (Saavana), (7) Chandra (Chaandra), (8) Arksa (Naakshatra) and (9) Guru (Baarhaspatya).
In this world, only 5 out of the 9 types are used for practical purpose. They are (1) Saura, (2) Savana, (3) Chaandra, (4) Arksha and (5) Baarhaspatya.
The 4 measures that are used in larger time measures i.e., that of Mahayugas etc. are (1) Braahma, (2) Daiva, (3) Maanva and (4) Pitrya.
Saura-maana: The Sidereal movement of Surya is called Sauramaana and is used for determining the motion of the planets. The Siddhanta Texts such as Surya Siddhanta, Pancha-Siddhantika etc. gives the mean revolutions of Grahas in a period of a Yuga or 43,20,000 Solar Revolutions with respect to the Nakshatras. From these the mean motion as well as the True motions of the Grahas can be found. Many confuse this with Tropical Year, which is incorrect as no Siddhantic Texts have mentioned computation of the Mean and True positions of the Grahas based on the Tropical Years.
Saavana-maana: Saavana day is the period between two successive Sunrises. This is almost equal to 24 hours or 60 Ghatikas. 360 such days make a Saavana year. The timing of the Rains and the period of pregnancy is to be known from the Saavana measure of time.
Naakshatra-maana: This reckoned based on the rotation of earth around its axis with respect to the Nakshatras. This is also called Sidereal Time. One complete rotation of Earth with respect to the Nakshatra is called a Sidereal Day. The mean Sidereal Day is 23.9344699 hours or 0.99726958 mean Solar days (Savana Days). Source Wikipedia. Where is this used? This is the Vedic Measure of a day of 60 Ghatikas and is identical with with the period between two successive rising of a Nakshatra. For instance, if someone is born in 10º Sagittarius, then for his One day is elapsed after Lagna reaches exactly at 10º Sagittarius the next day.
Chaandra-maana: This measure is based on the soli-lunar days or the Tithis. The Tithis are the distance of angular arc between Surya and Chandra. Chandra’s relative motion of 12º arc from Surya causes one Tithi. This is used in the timing of (1) Yagnopavita, Mundan etc. auspicious ceremonies, (2) Determination of the lord of the Tithi and the Lords of the Year, (3) Timing of fasting etc.
Baarhaspatya-maana: This measure is based on the mean motion of Guru. This is used in determine the effect of Samvatsaras such as Prabhava etc.
Each of these different time reckoning have their own specific Month and Year reckoning. For instance, in the Jovian calendar (Baarhaspatya maana), there are 60 years cycle starting from Prabhava etc.
Narada Purana 56.109. Measures of time and their use. Measures of time are nine, being, (1) Brahma, (2) Daiva, (3)Manu, (4) Pitrya, (5) Saura, (6) Savana, (7) Candra, (8) Arksa and (9) Guru.

Narada Purana 56.110. The Practical Measures. From among these nine measurers, practical reckoning is done only through five. The practical use of each of the time measures (maana) is set out here.

1. The Saura Maana (the Sidereal Year)
Narada Purana 56.111a. The motion of the planets is reckoned through the solar measures (saura-maana).

The Saura Maana is used for determining the Mean and True motions (positions) of the Grahas. One should refer to the Siddhantic texts such as Surya-Siddhanta, Pancha-Siddhantika etc.
Refer here for the definitions of various elements of a Yuga.
There are 4,320,000 Sidereal Years in a Mahayuga. It has 10 padas (foot) or 4,30,000 Sidereal Years. Kali Yuga is just 1 Pada of 4,30,000 Sidereal Years started on 22 Jan -3101 (3102 BC) as per proleptic Gregorian calendar.
Refer here for the mean motions of the Grahas that is defined based on the Mahayuga.
There is no doubt that Solar Years means Sidereal Solar Years and NOT Tropical Solar Years, as the planetary positions are not computed as per the Tropical Calendar in the Siddhantic Texts.
The Length of a day in this measure is motion of Surya by 1º.
2. The Savana Maana (the Civil Calendar)
Narada Purana 56.111b. The reckoning of the rainy season and pregnancy of women are reckoned through the civil measures (savana-maana).

The Savana Calendar is used for all practical purposes. In this calendar, the measure of a day is the period between two Sunrises (or Sunsets, Mid-days, Mid-nights).
This is equivalent to 24 hours and the Horas are defined based on this measure. The measures such as Ghatis, Vighatis, Kalas etc. are all defined based this measure of the day.
A Savana year is 360 Savana days having 12 months of 30 days each. However, the problem with this calendar is that, it gets out of sync with the Sidereal or Tropical Calendar, which are of 365+ days. Hence, to keep track of this calendar, the Aharganas are determined starting from the epoch, such as Kali Epoch or Varahamihira Epoch. The Agharganas ar the count of the Civil Days (Savana Days) starting from the Epoch.
The Lord of the Day (Vara) and Hora (Kshana Vara) is defined from this measure of the day.
Varahamihira recommends determining the lord of the year, month, week and day based on the Ahargana computation, which will be provided in another writeup.
Where it is used? This Time Measure finds wide usage for all day to day activities. In normal parlance a day is equivalent to the period of two successive Sunrises. The measure of Weeks (39 weeks) and Months (9 months) are used for determining the duration of pregnancy and labour.
It is also mentioned that the timing of Rain is done using this calendar. This implies that the Savana Calendar is synchronised with the Tropical Calendar. Perhaps that is the reason why the Tropical Calendar of 365.24216 Civil Days are not mentioned as one of the measure of time. Perhaps, this is due to that fact that the Tropical Year has the same definition of the Day as the Savana Day.
To Synchronise the Savana Calendar with the Tropical Calendar, the excess of 5.24216 years need to be adjusted, either by adding 5 extra days in the year. Or 30 extra days in 6 years. This will be discussed in a different place.
3. The Naakshatra-Maana (The Sidereal Day)
Narada Purana 56.112a. Within a year, the duration of a Ghatis etc. needs to be seen from the Naakshatra-Maana.

Surya Siddhanta 14.15. (1) The constant revolution of the circle of Nakshatras (Bhachakra) is called a Sidereal Day. (2) The months (for other measures) are to be known by the names of the Nakshatras, according to the conjunction at the end of a Lunar Period (Parvan.)

Everyday the nakshatras rise and set in the Eastern and Western Horizon respectively.
This is used for determining the Rasi (Zodiac Sign) Rising at the Horizon at the moment of birth.
This measure is useful in determining the Rising time of Various Nakshatras or the time to spot various Nakshatras on the Sky.
The mean sidereal day is 23 hours, 56 minutes, 4.0916 seconds (23.9344699 hours or 0.99726958 mean solar days)
The Duration of a Nakshatra Day is slightly shorter than a Civil Day. Why? The vernal equinox itself precesses slowly westward relative to the fixed stars, completing one revolution in about 26,000 years, so the sidereal day is some 0.0084 seconds shorter than the Earth’s period of rotation relative to the fixed stars
According to Wikipedia, Sidereal time is a time-keeping system astronomers use to keep track of the direction to point their telescopes to view a given star in the night sky. Briefly, sidereal time is a “time scale that is based on the Earth’s rate of rotation measured relative to the fixed stars” rather than the Sun.
More details can be found here in Wikipedia on the concept of Sidereal Time.
Some confuse this measure of time with Sidereal Year, however, that is not acceptable. Why? Because, (1) It is not based on the movement of Surya through the Nakshatras, (2) This is measure of a day and not year. Also, note Surya Siddhanta that mentions only the Sidereal Day and NOT sidereal year.
According to this writeup, there are 1,582,237,500 revolution of the Nakshatra in a Mahayuga of 4,320,000 Sidereal Years. In the same period, the number of Civil Days (Savana Dina) is 1,577,917,500. This means that 1 Sidereal Day = 0.99726968928495 Civil Day (Mean Solar Day). The modern value for this conversion is 0.99726958, which is very close to what suggested by the Siddhanta Texts.
4. The Chaandra Maana (The Lunar Measure)
Narada Purana 56.112b-113a. Times for Yagnopavita (Sacred thread ceremony), Mundan etc. auspicious ceremonies, determination of the lords of the Tithi and the Year, timing of fasting etc. are done using the lunar measure (chaandra-maana).

There are two elements of Lunar Time Measure, that is very useful in determining auspicious time for various Samskaras such as Yagnopavita, Mundan etc. These are Tithi and Karana. These two are important elements of Panchanga, the 5 elements of Time.
The Tithis are formed due to the relative motion of Chandra from Surya.
The Tithis are grouped into Nanda etc. which are used for determining auspicious time in Muhurtha.
The Lunar Calendar starts with the Shukla Chaitra Pratipada, which happens at the moment of conjunction of Surya and Chandra in Sidereal Pisces.
Each Tithi is given a specific name to illustrate the quality of its energy and vibrations.
The Tithis are grouped in Shukla and Krsna Paksha which are used for timing various activitirs. It is know that the Shukla Paksha belongs to the Devas while the Krsna Paksha belongs to the Pitris (manes, forefathers).
The observance of certain Vrats (fasting etc.), festivals such as Ratha Saptami, Krsna Astami, Rama Navami etc. are done based on the Tithis coinciding with certain Lunar months.
The Lunar Months start with the Sun-Moon conjunction in every Zodiac signs. If there are more than one conjunction, the Month initiated by the 2nd conjunction is considered as the Real Month, while the month arising out of the previous conjunction is called Extra Month (Adhika Masa, Mala Masa, Purushottama Masa).
5. The Baarhaspatya Maana (The Jovian Years)
There are 60 Jovian Years starting from Prabhava and ending with Ksaya.
The year is defined based on the Mean Motion of Guru.
This is based on the rule that, in a Mahayuga of 4,320,000 Solar Years, there ar 4,370,688 Jovian years.
Refer here for details of the computation of the Jovian years.
The same year naming is used for Lunar Years in the Salivahana Era or even in the Solar Calendar that starts with Sun’s movement into Sidereal Mesha Rasi. But these years are different from the Jovian years. The Jovian years are almost 12 years advanced than those followed in the Saka or Vikram Samvat Era.
The Jovian Years should be really used to determine the state of the world and not the ones determined by the other Eras. More to be covered in future writeup.

The Definitions of the Ayana and the Seasons
The Tropical Calendar was known from ancient ages, and was used for determination of the Season.
The determination of the Sun’s Northerly and Southerly course (Uttarayana, Dakshinayanas and the Seasons are based on the Tropical movement of Surya. There is however, difference in opinion regarding using the Ayana Movement and the Seasons in the Sidereal Zodiac for analysis of Horoscopes (and not seasons).
Narada Purana 56.125. Uttarayana and Dakshinayana. Reckoning from the commencement of Capricorn, six months, in the solar measure constitute Uttarayana (the northward course of the Sun); this is also the day time of the gods. (The six months) beginning from cancer is Dakshinayana (southward course of the Sun) and that forms the night (of the gods).

Narada Purana 56.126–127a. Performance of auspicious ceremonies During Uttarayana should be performed all such auspicious rites as the first entry into the house, marriage, consecration of idols, ritual tying of the girdle made of munja grass, fire sacrifices etc. (yajnadi). During Dakshinayana is to be done all inauspicious acts.

Narada Purana 56.127b-128. The seasons. Two months each, as reckoned from Magha constitute the (six) seasons Sisira etc. Thus, during Uttarayana occur (the three seasons) Sisira (cool), Vasanta (Spring) and Grisma (hot), commencing from Capricorn. And, during Daksinayana occur (the three seasons) Varsa (rainy), Sarad (autumn) and Hemanta (winter).

The Naming of the Months
The basis for starting of the Lunar months and the Solar months, and their naming is given here.
The Lunar months are derived based on the expected position of Chandra at the moment of Purnima in a specific month. For instance, assuming that the Chaitra Month started with Sun-Moon conjunction in the middle of Meena Rasi, the Purnima will falls at the starting point of Mesha, coinciding with the Chitra Nakshatra, hence the month name is given as Chaitra.
Narada Purana 56.129. The Solar and Lunar months. Lunar months are reckoned up to the end of the new moon and solar months from Sankranti (i.e., entry of the Sun into the relevant signs of the Zodiac). In 30 days would one revolution of the Moon be completed, as also one sidereal (nakshatra) month.

Narada Purana 56.130–131a. The names of the 12 months. The names of the 12 months from Caitra are, in order : (1) Madhu, (2) Madhava, (3) Sukra, (4) Suci, (5) Nabha, (6) Nabhasya, (7) Isa, (8) Urja, (9) Saha, (10) Sahasyaka, (11) Tapa and (12) Tapasyaka.

Narada Purana 56.131b-132a. Basis of the Naming of the Months. That constellation in which the full moon occurs in any month, that month is named after that constellation. That full moon is also named with (the name of the constellation tacked to it).

Narada Purana 56.132b-133a. The Pakshas. The fortnights. The two fortnights, viz., Sukla (bright) and krsna (dark) of the month are termed Daiva (pertaining to the gods) and Pitrya (pertaining to the manes). The two are specified for the performance of auspicious and inauspicious rites, respectively.
