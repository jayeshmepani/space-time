**The sage-attributed (Arsha) Hindu units of time, drawn exclusively from critical classical texts like the Srimad Bhagavatam (Bhagavata Purana, Canto 3, Chapter 11 — “Calculation of Time, from the Atom”), attributed to Sage Vyasa (Krishna Dvaipayana Vyasa, a Vedic Rishi and compiler of the Puranas).** This is the primary source providing the smallest unit starting with *Paramanu*, as you mentioned. Complementary details on larger units align closely with other Arsha texts, such as the Vishnu Purana (narrated by Sage Parashara) and Manusmriti (attributed to Sage Manu), which describe the human-scale units without contradicting the core hierarchy.

These texts treat time (*kāla*) as a divine potency of the Supreme (often linked to the movement of atoms under the sun’s rays or cosmic order), revealed through sages rather than historical astronomers. The system is consistent across these sources for units up to one human year (*samvatsara* or *varṣa*, conventionally 360 days in these classical descriptions: 12 months of 30 days each). Variations in exact multipliers for the tiniest units exist between texts, but the Bhagavata Purana gives the most detailed atomic-level breakdown you referenced.

### Hierarchical List of Time Units (Smallest to 1 Human Year)
Units build logarithmically. I list each with its definition/multiplier from the Bhagavata Purana (Vyasa), followed by approximate modern equivalent (for context only; classical texts do not use seconds but describe via atomic motion or physiological phenomena like eye-blinks). The chain reaches the human year explicitly as one day-and-night cycle of the demigods (*devas*).

- **Paramāṇu** (परमाणु) — Base atomic unit: time taken by the sun to traverse one indivisible atomic particle (the ultimate subtle particle of matter).  
- **Aṇu** (अणु) — 2 Paramāṇu (double atom).  
- **Trasareṇu** (त्रसरेणु, also called hexatom) — 3 Aṇu (combination of 6 atoms; visible as dust motes in a sunbeam).  
- **Truṭi** (त्रुटि) — Time for integration/combination of 3 Trasareṇu.  
- **Vedha** (वेध) — 100 Truṭi.  
- **Lava** (लव) — 3 Vedha.  
- **Nimeṣa** (निमेष, blink/twinkling of an eye) — 3 Lava.  
- **Kṣaṇa** (क्षण) — 3 Nimeṣa.  
- **Kāṣṭhā** (काष्ठा) — 5 Kṣaṇa.  
- **Laghu** (लघु) — 15 Kāṣṭhā.  
- **Nāḍikā** (नाडिका) or **Daṇḍa** (दण्ड) — 15 Laghu (≈24 minutes; classically measured by a water-clock: a copper vessel with a hole, calibrated to a specific weight and probe).  
- **Muhūrta** (मुहूर्त) — 2 Nāḍikā/Daṇḍa (≈48 minutes).  
- **Ahorātra** (अहोरात्र, day + night for humans/mortals) — 30 Muhūrta (full 24-hour cycle; 360 days make a year in these texts).  
- **Pakṣa** (पक्ष, fortnight) — 15 Ahorātra (15 days + nights; bright/dark halves).  
- **Māsa** (मास, month) — 2 Pakṣa (30 days).  
- **Ṛtu** (ऋतु, season) — 2 Māsa (2 months).  
- **Ayana** (अयन, half-year: Uttarāyaṇa or Dakṣiṇāyana, sun’s northward/southward path) — 3 Ṛtu (6 months).  
- **Samvatsara** / **Varṣa** (संवत्सर / वर्ष, human year) — 2 Ayana (360 days; explicitly one complete day-and-night of the demigods in the Bhagavata Purana and aligned texts). One human year equals the time for the sun to complete its full cycle through the seasons.

### Key Notes from the Classical Sage-Attributed Texts
- **Source primacy**: The atomic-to-muhūrta chain is directly from Bhagavata Purana 3.11 (Vyasa). Larger units (pakṣa to samvatsara) are consistent with Vishnu Purana (Parashara: “Fifteen twinklings of the eye make a Kāṣṭhā; thirty Kāṣṭhās, one Kalā; thirty Kalās, one Muhūrta. Thirty Muhūrtas make one day… thirty days a month; twelve months a year”) and Manusmriti 1.64 (Manu: similar nimeṣa-based structure with 18 nimeṣa = 1 kāṣṭhā). Mahabharata (Vyasa) also references these for ritual and cosmological timing.  
- **360-day year**: All these Arsha texts treat the civil/human year as 360 days (12 × 30), with adjustments for lunar/solar alignment noted separately in ritual contexts. This matches your specification.  
- **No historical/Paurusheya texts included**: I excluded later works (e.g., Surya Siddhanta commentaries or medieval astronomers) as per your definition. These units are prehistoric/legendary, revealed to or compiled by Ṛṣis/Avatars.  
- **Philosophical context**: Time begins at the atomic level (sun traversing paramāṇu) and scales to cosmic perception (e.g., 1 human year = 1 deva day-and-night). Different beings experience time differently (e.g., pitṛs, devas, Brahmā).
